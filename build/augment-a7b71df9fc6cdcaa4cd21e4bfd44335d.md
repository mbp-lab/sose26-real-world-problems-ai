<details>
<summary><strong> Image augmentation </strong></summary>

For image augmentation, the simplest forms are geometric augmentation, such as rotations, translation, flipping, cropping and similar operations. Photographic transformations introduce shifts in color, brightness, contrast, add noise to the pixels, et cetera. It may also be helpful to randomly erase or occlude parts of pictures: think of street signs covered with graffiti - they should still be recognizable, for example to self-driving cars. More advanced augmentation methods combine different images in various ways. Let's informally call them "Frankenstein" or "ghost" augmentations.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong> Text augmentation </strong></summary>

When working with textual data, several methods can be applied for augmentation. Broadly, they can be divided into character, word and sentence-level augmentations.

Character augmentations include introducing typos in various ways, such as replacing the letter o with zero, or I with 1. A practical use case for this could be hate speech detection. For example, some people actively introduce such letter changes to bypass different community violations and augmentations that are trained on similar alterations, can help with that. Other character-level augmentations include keyboard augmentations, where characters are replaced by neighboring characters on a keyboard. 

![Image](images/text-augment)

Word augmentations introduce spelling mistakes. These are different from typos because they include common orthographic mistakes, not typos, which can be random or based on things like keyboard adjacency. Other methods include splitting words into tokens, substituting words by similar words, deleting or swapping them. 

Sentence augmentations may involve generative AI to replace entire sentences or parts thereof with sentences with similar meaning. 

Image Source: https://amitness.com/posts/data-augmentation-for-nlp


</details>


<details>
<summary><strong> Audio augmentation </strong></summary>

Audio data can be modified by changing the properties of the sound wave. For example, one can speed up or speed down the audio signal (time stretching), introduce artificial breaks, such as pauses (time shifting). It is also possible to change the frequency and altitude of the sound, inject noise, filter specific frequencies. In addition to the augmentations applied to the raw sound, audio data can be represented as a spectrogram and similar manipulations may be applied on the spectrogram images. 

![Image](images/audio-augment)

</details>


<details>
<summary><strong> Tabular data augmentation </strong></summary>

Tabular data can be more challenging to augment. Many methods for tabular data augmentation appear to be related to general data processing, feature engineering and dimensionality reduction metrics. Specifically, the book "Data Augmentation with Python" book lists methods, such as scaling of column values, binning or categorical encoding, outlier detection and removal. Further, new features can be generated by exploiting the correlations between already existing features, performing simple operations between values of different columns or projecting data into lower dimensions, for example through eigendecomopsition. 


</details>

<details>
<summary><strong> Caution! </strong></summary>

As with any method, data augmentation should be applied with caution and with a strong focus on the task at hand. Not all transformations are appropriate for every application. For instance, for hand-written digit recognition, flipping 6 and 9 should introduce label changes. In medical imaging, such as skin cancer detection, certain augmentations may alter or obscure diagnostically relevant features (shape, edges of a lesion, color, size) and thus lead to incorrect predictions. In text data, even small changes can significantly affect meaning, for instance, the addition or removal of a word like "not" can completely reverse the intended interpretation. Similarly, in audio processing, augmentations that improve robustness for speech recognition in general (e.g. changes in speed or pitch) may be detrimental in tasks such as emotion recognition, where these attributes carry important semantic information.

Another critical consideration is the prevention of data leakage. This occurs when information from the validation or test set influences the training process, leading to overly optimistic performance. In the context of data augmentation, leakage can arise if augmented samples derived from the same original instance appear across both training and evaluation splits. As a result, the model may effectively "see" parts of the test data during training. 

</details>
